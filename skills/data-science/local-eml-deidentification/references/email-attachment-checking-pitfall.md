# Attachment-Checking Pitfall: Loan Agreement vs Personal Payments

## What Happened

In the BMM/Worner analysis session, the email chain from BMM (Jasper, 22 May 2026) mentioned that Eric's personal payments to Diluca would be "treated as an interest-bearing loan." The initial analysis incorrectly characterised the 9% interest rate amendment as benefiting Eric personally.

## The Error

The reasoning was: "Eric paid Diluca expenses → BMM proposed raising the interest rate from 6% to 9% → the higher rate must benefit Eric."

**This was wrong.** The attached loan agreement (`Additional Agreement No 1 to LA (Diluca) copy`) showed that the 9% amendment was on the **Smith Family Trust's shareholder loan to Diluca** — the clients' own loan — not on Eric's personal payments. The 9% rate benefits the clients as lenders, not Eric.

## Root Cause

The analysis was done from the **email body text only**, without first opening and reading the referenced attachment (the Additional Agreement PDF). The email body said "loan payable to Eric" in one paragraph and "9% interest rate" in another, but never explicitly stated which loan the 9% applied to.

## Rule for Future Sessions

**Before characterising any financial arrangement or conflict of interest mentioned in an email, check for referenced attachments first.** Emails often summarise or hint at terms that are fully defined only in the attached document. Specific steps:

1. Look for attachment references in the email (PDF, .docx, .xlsx file mentions)
2. Open and read the referenced document before writing any analysis about:
   - Who benefits from a proposed change
   - Which entity or person a rate/term applies to
   - The nature of a payment (loan vs expense vs distribution)
3. Quote the relevant clause from the document, not just the email summary

## Example

When an email says: "The payments will be reflected as a loan payable to Eric" AND "the interest rate will be increased from 6% to 9%", don't assume these refer to the same loan. Check the attached agreement to see if the 9% applies to a **different** loan (e.g. the shareholder loan from the trust, not Eric's personal advances).

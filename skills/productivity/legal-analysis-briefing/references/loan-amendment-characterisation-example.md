# Real-World Example: The 9% Loan Amendment

This is a documented example of why you must always read referenced attachments before characterising them.

## The Scenario

In a de-identified email chain about Greek property investments, BMM (the project manager) asked the client to sign an "Additional Agreement" increasing the shareholder loan interest rate from 6% to 9%.

The email summarised the amendment and asked for a decision. The analysis initially assumed the 9% rate was a self-serving move that benefited Eric (BMM's principal) because:

- Eric had made personal payments to cover Diluca expenses
- The email mentioned Eric's payments being treated as loans
- The timing was suspicious (raised while client funds were frozen)

## The Reality

The attached **Additional Agreement No 1 to Loan Agreement** showed:

- **Lender:** Worner Family Holdings Pty Ltd (the client's trust)
- **Borrower:** Diluca Limited (the Greek operating entity)
- **Purpose:** Amend the interest rate on the **client's** shareholder loan to Diluca from 6% to 9%

The 9% rate benefited the **client** as lender, not Eric. It was a legitimate commercial negotiation (Trident had previously forced the rate down to 6%; BMM negotiated it back up to 9%).

## The Lesson

When an email says "please see attached" or "I have attached" — ALWAYS:

1. Find the attachment file on disk
2. Open and read it (via pdftotext or text extraction)
3. Check: who are the parties? What is the actual change being made?
4. Compare the email's summary to the document's text — do they match?
5. Only then characterise the document in your analysis

This applies to: loan agreements, fee schedules, management accounts, invoices, bank statements, resolutions, incorporation documents, and any other legal/financial attachments.
